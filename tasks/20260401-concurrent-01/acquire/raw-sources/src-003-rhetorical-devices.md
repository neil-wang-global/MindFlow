# Figures of Speech: A Taxonomy of Rhetorical Devices

Rhetorical devices (also called figures of speech or rhetorical figures) are techniques that writers and speakers use to achieve particular effects beyond the literal meaning of their words. The classical taxonomy of rhetorical devices distinguishes between figures of speech (figurative language) and figures of thought (arrangement and emphasis).

## Figures of Comparison (Tropes)

### Metaphor
A metaphor is an implicit comparison between two unlike things, asserting that one thing *is* another. Unlike simile, metaphor does not use "like" or "as." Aristotle called metaphor "the mark of genius" in the *Poetics*, arguing that the ability to perceive resemblances between dissimilar things is the most important skill for a writer.

**Example**: "All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players." — Shakespeare, *As You Like It*

Metaphor operates by transferring qualities from the "vehicle" (the image or concept borrowed) to the "tenor" (the subject being described). Extended metaphors sustain the comparison across multiple sentences or an entire work.

### Simile
A simile is an explicit comparison using "like" or "as." While related to metaphor, simile maintains the distinction between the two things being compared.

**Example**: "Her eyes were like stars" uses "like" to signal the comparison.

### Metonymy
Metonymy substitutes the name of something with the name of something closely associated with it. "The pen is mightier than the sword" uses "pen" for writing/words and "sword" for military force.

## Figures of Repetition

### Anaphora
Anaphora is the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses, sentences, or lines. It creates rhythm and emphasis, building rhetorical momentum.

**Example**: "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets." — Winston Churchill

Anaphora is one of the most powerful devices in persuasive oratory, as the repeated phrase becomes a rhythmic anchor that reinforces the speaker's central message.

### Epistrophe
Epistrophe (also called epiphora) is the repetition of a word or phrase at the *end* of successive clauses. It is the mirror of anaphora.

**Example**: "...of the people, by the people, for the people." — Abraham Lincoln

## Figures of Balance

### Parallelism
Parallelism is the use of similar grammatical structures in related words, phrases, or clauses. It creates a sense of balance, rhythm, and clarity. Parallelism operates at multiple levels: word-level, phrase-level, clause-level, and sentence-level.

**Example**: "I came, I saw, I conquered." — Julius Caesar (tricolon with parallel structure)

Parallelism is fundamental to effective prose. It aids comprehension by establishing predictable patterns and creates an aesthetic sense of order.

### Antithesis
Antithesis places two contrasting ideas in parallel structure, typically within a single sentence. The juxtaposition of opposites creates emphasis and clarity.

**Example**: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times." — Charles Dickens

**Example**: "That's one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind." — Neil Armstrong

Antithesis works by making contrasts explicit through structural symmetry. The parallel grammar forces the reader to compare the opposing ideas directly.

### Chiasmus
Chiasmus reverses the grammatical structure of a parallel phrase, creating an A-B-B-A pattern.

**Example**: "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country." — John F. Kennedy
