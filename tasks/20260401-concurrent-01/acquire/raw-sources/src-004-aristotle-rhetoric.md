# Aristotle's Rhetoric

*First published Tue May 2, 2002; substantive revision Thu Feb 1, 2022*

Aristotle's *Rhetoric* is an ancient Greek treatise on the art of persuasion, dating from the fourth century BCE. It is the most important single work on persuasion ever written.

## 1. The Subject and Purpose of Rhetoric

Aristotle defines rhetoric as "the faculty of observing in any given case the available means of persuasion" (*Rhetoric* I.2, 1355b25). This definition frames rhetoric not as the art of speaking well per se but as the systematic study of how persuasion works.

## 3. Style: Making a Speech Clear and Appropriate

Aristotle devotes Book III of the *Rhetoric* to style (lexis), which he defines as the manner in which the orator's ideas are expressed. He distinguishes between clarity and appropriateness as the two essential virtues of style.

### Metaphor as the Central Rhetorical Figure

For Aristotle, metaphor is the most important rhetorical figure. He writes: "The greatest thing by far is to be a master of metaphor. It is the one thing that cannot be learned from others; and it is also a sign of genius, since a good metaphor implies an intuitive perception of the similarity in dissimilars" (*Poetics* 1459a).

In the *Rhetoric* (III.2), Aristotle provides a taxonomy of metaphor types based on the logical relationship between the two terms:

1. Genus to species
2. Species to genus
3. Species to species
4. Analogy (proportional metaphor)

The analogical metaphor (type 4) is, for Aristotle, the most effective: "A is to B as C is to D; one may then speak of D as B or B as D" (Rhetoric III.4, 1407a).

### Antithesis

Aristotle identifies antithesis as a particularly effective structural device: "This style is pleasing, because contraries are easily understood and even more so when placed side by side" (*Rhetoric* III.9, 1410a). He notes that antithetical statements are concise, balanced, and create a sense of completeness.

### Rhythm and Prose Style

Aristotle argues that prose should have rhythm but not meter. While poetry uses regular metrical patterns (such as iambic pentameter), prose benefits from rhythmic variation that feels natural. He recommends the paean as the most suitable prose rhythm because it is the least recognizable as verse.
