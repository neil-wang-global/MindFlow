# Narrative Structure | Literature & Writing

**Narrative structure**, in literature, the framework of a narrative — the sequence and manner in which events are presented to the reader or audience. It encompasses both the chronological order of events (the "fabula" or story) and the way they are arranged in the text (the "syuzhet" or plot), a distinction formalized by Russian Formalist critics in the early twentieth century.

## Historical Development

The study of narrative structure traces to Aristotle's *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE), where he identified the fundamental components of dramatic plot: beginning (protasis), middle (epitasis), and end (catastrophe). Aristotle argued that a well-constructed plot must have unity of action — a single, complete action with a clear beginning, middle, and end, where every element contributes to the whole.

The Roman poet Horace, in his *Ars Poetica* (c. 19 BCE), introduced the concept of beginning *in medias res* — "in the middle of things" — recommending that epic poets begin their narratives amid the action rather than from the chronological start.

## Structural Models

Several formal models of narrative structure have been developed:

**Linear structure**: events are presented in chronological order from beginning to end. This is the most straightforward approach and is common in realist fiction.

**Non-linear structure**: events are presented out of chronological order. Techniques include flashbacks (analepsis), flash-forwards (prolepsis), and fragmented timelines.

**Frame narrative**: a story is told within a story, where an outer narrative frames the inner narrative. Examples include *The Canterbury Tales* and *Wuthering Heights*.

**Circular structure**: the narrative ends where it began, often suggesting cyclical themes or the futility of change.

The three-act structure — setup, confrontation, and resolution — remains the dominant model in commercial fiction and screenwriting, though literary fiction frequently subverts or complicates this framework.

## Narrative Perspective and Voice

Narrative structure is inseparable from narrative voice (first person, second person, third person limited, third person omniscient) and focalization (the perspective through which events are perceived). The choice of narrative voice affects how information is revealed to the reader and the degree of interpretive authority the narrator holds.
