# Persuasion | Psychology & Rhetoric

**Persuasion**, the process by which a person's attitudes or behaviour are, without duress, influenced by communications from other people. One's attitudes and behaviour are also affected by other factors — for example, verbal threats, physical coercion, and one's physiological states. Not all communication is intended to be persuasive; other purposes include informing or entertaining.

## Aristotle's Framework

The systematic study of persuasion begins with Aristotle's *Rhetoric* (4th century BCE), which identified three modes of proof (pisteis): **ethos** (the character of the speaker), **pathos** (the emotional state of the audience), and **logos** (the argument itself). This tripartite framework remains the foundation of rhetorical theory.

Aristotle considered rhetoric the counterpart (antistrophos) of dialectic — both are concerned with probable reasoning rather than demonstration. While dialectic seeks truth through structured dialogue, rhetoric seeks persuasion through public discourse.

## Modern Persuasion Theory

### The Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)

Developed by psychologists Richard Petty and John Cacioppo (1986), the ELM proposes two routes to persuasion:

- **Central route**: the audience carefully evaluates the quality of the arguments. This route produces more durable attitude change.
- **Peripheral route**: the audience relies on superficial cues such as the attractiveness of the speaker, the number of arguments, or social proof. This route produces less stable attitude change.

The ELM maps roughly onto Aristotle's appeals: the central route corresponds primarily to logos (argument quality), while the peripheral route incorporates elements of ethos (source credibility) and pathos (emotional cues).

### Cialdini's Principles of Influence

Robert Cialdini (1984) identified six principles of social influence:

1. **Reciprocity**: people feel obligated to return favours
2. **Commitment and consistency**: people seek to be consistent with their prior commitments
3. **Social proof**: people look to others' behaviour to determine appropriate action
4. **Authority**: people defer to perceived experts
5. **Liking**: people are more easily persuaded by those they like
6. **Scarcity**: people value things that are rare or diminishing in availability

These principles complement Aristotle's framework by providing empirically validated mechanisms through which ethos, pathos, and logos operate in practice.

## Persuasion in Writing

In written communication, persuasion relies primarily on:
- **Thesis clarity**: stating the central claim explicitly
- **Evidence marshalling**: presenting credible evidence in a logical sequence
- **Counterargument acknowledgment**: addressing opposing views (a form of ethos-building)
- **Rhetorical arrangement**: organizing the argument for maximum impact (often following classical arrangement: exordium, narratio, confirmatio, refutatio, peroratio)
