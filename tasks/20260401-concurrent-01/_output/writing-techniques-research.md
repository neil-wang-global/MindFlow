## 1. Narrative Structure Techniques

*Sources: src-001 (Purdue OWL), src-002 (Encyclopaedia Britannica)*

### 1.1 The Three-Act Structure

The three-act structure is the most widely used narrative framework in Western storytelling. It divides a narrative into three parts:

- **Act I (Setup/Exposition)**: Introduces the protagonist, setting, and central conflict. Establishes the "normal world" before the inciting incident disrupts equilibrium.
- **Act II (Confrontation/Rising Action)**: The longest act. The protagonist faces escalating obstacles while pursuing their goal. The midpoint raises the stakes; the act ends with a crisis or "dark moment."
- **Act III (Resolution/Climax and Denouement)**: The climax resolves the central conflict. The denouement ties up remaining threads and establishes a new equilibrium.

This framework remains the dominant model in commercial fiction and screenwriting, though literary fiction frequently subverts or complicates it.

### 1.2 In Medias Res

In medias res ("into the middle of things") is a technique in which the story begins at a midpoint or climactic moment rather than the chronological beginning. First described by Horace in *Ars Poetica* (c. 19 BCE), who praised Homer for beginning the *Iliad* amid the Trojan War.

Key characteristics:
- Opens with action, conflict, or dramatic tension
- Defers exposition to later in the narrative
- Relies on flashbacks or gradual revelation
- Creates immediacy and urgency

### 1.3 Freytag's Pyramid

Gustav Freytag developed a five-part dramatic structure:
1. **Exposition**: Introduction of characters, setting, background
2. **Rising Action**: Complications that build tension
3. **Climax**: The moment of greatest tension
4. **Falling Action**: Events leading toward resolution
5. **Denouement**: Final resolution and new equilibrium

Originally for classical drama analysis, now applied across prose fiction, screenwriting, and other narrative forms.

### 1.4 Story Arcs

A story arc traces the trajectory of transformation across a narrative:
- **The Hero's Journey** (Joseph Campbell): circular arc of departure, trials, and return
- **Rags to Riches**: rise from poverty/obscurity to success
- **Tragedy**: fall from strength due to a fatal flaw
- **Rebirth**: fundamental transformation in worldview or character

### 1.5 Other Structural Models

- **Linear structure**: chronological presentation
- **Non-linear structure**: flashbacks (analepsis), flash-forwards (prolepsis), fragmented timelines
- **Frame narrative**: story within a story
- **Circular structure**: narrative ends where it began

The Russian Formalist distinction between *fabula* (the chronological story) and *syuzhet* (the arrangement in the text) provides a theoretical foundation for understanding how narrative structure shapes reader experience.

### 1.6 Historical Foundation

The study of narrative structure traces to Aristotle's *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE), which identified the fundamental components: beginning (protasis), middle (epitasis), and end (catastrophe). Aristotle argued for unity of action -- every element must contribute to the whole.

## 2. Rhetorical Devices

*Sources: src-003 (BYU Rhetoric Database), src-004 (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy)*

### 2.1 Figures of Comparison (Tropes)

**Metaphor**: An implicit comparison asserting that one thing IS another, without "like" or "as." Aristotle called it "the mark of genius" -- the ability to perceive resemblances between dissimilar things. Operates by transferring qualities from the "vehicle" (borrowed image) to the "tenor" (subject described). Extended metaphors sustain the comparison across multiple sentences or an entire work.

Aristotle's taxonomy of metaphor types:
1. Genus to species
2. Species to genus
3. Species to species
4. Analogy (proportional metaphor -- the most effective)

**Simile**: An explicit comparison using "like" or "as." Maintains the distinction between the two things compared.

**Metonymy**: Substituting a name with something closely associated. "The pen is mightier than the sword" uses "pen" for writing and "sword" for military force.

### 2.2 Figures of Repetition

**Anaphora**: Repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses, sentences, or lines. Creates rhythm and emphasis, building rhetorical momentum. One of the most powerful devices in persuasive oratory. Example: "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets" (Churchill).

**Epistrophe** (epiphora): Repetition at the end of successive clauses -- the mirror of anaphora. Example: "...of the people, by the people, for the people" (Lincoln).

### 2.3 Figures of Balance

**Parallelism**: Use of similar grammatical structures in related words, phrases, or clauses. Creates balance, rhythm, and clarity. Operates at word, phrase, clause, and sentence levels. Fundamental to effective prose -- aids comprehension through predictable patterns and creates aesthetic order. Example: "I came, I saw, I conquered" (tricolon with parallel structure).

**Antithesis**: Places two contrasting ideas in parallel structure within a single sentence. Creates emphasis through juxtaposition of opposites. Aristotle: "contraries are easily understood and even more so when placed side by side." Examples: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" (Dickens); "That's one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind" (Armstrong).

**Chiasmus**: Reverses the grammatical structure of a parallel phrase, creating an A-B-B-A pattern. Example: "Ask not what your country can do for you -- ask what you can do for your country" (Kennedy).

### 2.4 Prose Style

Aristotle argues that prose should have rhythm but not meter. While poetry uses regular metrical patterns, prose benefits from rhythmic variation that feels natural.

## 3. Persuasion Techniques

*Sources: src-005 (Purdue OWL)*

### 3.1 Ethos (Credibility)

Persuasive appeal based on the character and credibility of the speaker/writer. Established through:
- **Demonstrating expertise**: accurate information, appropriate terminology, citation of authorities
- **Establishing common ground**: connecting with the audience's values and experiences
- **Showing fairness**: acknowledging counterarguments rather than dismissing them
- **Maintaining consistency**: ensuring internal coherence of argument and tone

Aristotle distinguished "prior ethos" (existing reputation) from "invented ethos" (credibility established within the text). In writing, invented ethos is particularly important because readers often lack prior knowledge of the writer.

### 3.2 Pathos (Emotional Appeal)

Appeal to the audience's emotions. Aristotle: "The orator persuades by means of his hearers, when they are roused to emotion by his speech."

Effective use includes:
- **Vivid language and imagery**: concrete sensory experiences evoking emotional responses
- **Narrative and anecdote**: stories that engage emotionally
- **Tone and word choice**: words with appropriate emotional connotations
- **Appeals to values**: connecting to the audience's deeply held beliefs

Important distinction: legitimate emotional appeals illuminate the significance of an issue; fallacious emotional appeals substitute emotion for evidence.

### 3.3 Logos (Logical Appeal)

Appeal based on logic and reasoning, encompassing both argument structure and evidence:
- **Deductive reasoning**: general premises to specific conclusions (syllogisms)
- **Inductive reasoning**: specific examples to general conclusions
- **Evidence**: facts, statistics, expert testimony, research findings
- **Logical structure**: clear, progressive organization

The enthymeme (rhetorical syllogism with an unstated premise) is what Aristotle called "the body of persuasion" -- it engages the audience by requiring them to supply the missing premise.

### 3.4 Kairos (Timeliness)

Increasingly recognized as a fourth dimension of rhetorical strategy, kairos addresses the question of WHEN to make an argument:
- Current context and its relevance
- The audience's readiness to receive the message
- Appropriateness of medium and format

## Cross-Domain Connections

The three sub-domains of writing techniques are deeply interconnected:

1. **Narrative structure provides the scaffold for rhetorical devices**: The three-act structure creates the context in which devices like anaphora, antithesis, and metaphor achieve maximum impact. The climax of Act III is where rhetorical intensity peaks.

2. **Rhetorical devices serve persuasion**: Metaphor, parallelism, and antithesis are not merely ornamental -- they are mechanisms of persuasion. Antithesis makes contrasts explicit (logos), anaphora builds emotional momentum (pathos), and well-chosen metaphors demonstrate mastery of the subject (ethos).

3. **Persuasion shapes narrative choices**: The Aristotelian appeals inform narrative decisions. A narrator's credibility (ethos) is established through narrative voice. Emotional engagement (pathos) is achieved through story arcs like tragedy or rebirth. The logical structure of the plot (logos) determines whether the narrative feels coherent and inevitable.
