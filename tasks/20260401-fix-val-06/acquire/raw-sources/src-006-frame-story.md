## Original Content

Definition: A frame story (also called frame tale, framing device, frame narrative, sandwich narrative, or intercalation) is a literary technique in which an introductory or main narrative establishes the setting and context for one or more embedded tales. The outer narrative leads readers into the inner narratives and can supply information that clarifies them. Described as "Story in a nested narration that brackets one or more embedded stories."

Origins and history:
- Early instances in antiquity: ancient Egypt (Papyrus Westcar; Tale of the Shipwrecked Sailor; The Eloquent Peasant) and early Indian literature (Mahabharata, Ramayana, Panchatantra, Hitopadesha, Baital Pachisi / Vikram and the Vampire).
- Classical and medieval Western uses: Homer's Odyssey (Odysseus telling stories to Alcinous).
- Notable historic collections: One Thousand and One Nights, The Decameron, Canterbury Tales.

Representative examples:
- One Thousand and One Nights: Scheherazade frames a large set of tales, many containing their own frames (e.g., Sindbad).
- The Decameron and Canterbury Tales: pilgrim/storyteller frameworks organizing individual stories.
- Frankenstein: nested multi-frame — Robert Walton's letters frame Victor Frankenstein's account, which contains the creature's own narrated history.
- Additional: Ovid's Metamorphoses, Wuthering Heights, The Tenant of Wildfell Hall, Neil Gaiman's The Sandman.
- Film: Forrest Gump, Slumdog Millionaire.

Structural mechanics and uses:
- Organizing a set of stories: a frame provides a conceit to collect shorter narratives; can create mise en abyme when embedded tale reflects the frame.
- Positioning reader attitude: frame can set expectations or distance author from storyteller, indicating possible unreliability.
- Casting doubt on narrator: authors make narrator a character in the frame to invite skepticism (e.g., Mr. Mulliner tales).
- Procatalepsis: frame presents listeners voicing anticipated reader reactions (e.g., The Princess Bride's grandfather/grandson frame).
- False documents: frames use "false documents" (letters, transcripts, archival notes) to explain how inner narrative was found (e.g., Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde; The Handmaid's Tale epilogue).
- Dream vision: narrator claims to have dreamed the story; common in medieval literature.
- Reprise: recurring imagery or scenes at beginning and end bracket the main narrative.
