## Original Content

Definition and origin: Chekhov's gun is a narrative principle that "every element in a story [be] necessary, while irrelevant elements should be removed." It is recorded in several letters by Anton Chekhov as advice to young playwrights (Chekhov letters cited, e.g. 1 November 1889).

Direct quotations attributed to Chekhov and immediate variants:
- "One must never place a loaded rifle on the stage if it isn't going to go off. It's wrong to make promises you don't mean to keep." — cited from Chekhov (letter to Aleksandr Semenovich Lazarev, 1 November 1889).
- Variant attributed in Sergius Shchukin's 1911 Memoirs: "If you say in the first act that there is a rifle hanging on the wall, in the second or third act it absolutely must go off. If it's not going to be fired, it shouldn't be hanging there."
- Earlier formulation (cited 1904, Ilia Gurliand): "If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired. Otherwise don't put it there."

Principle and later meaning: Chekhov's original intent emphasizes narrative economy and necessity: elements introduced should have dramatic purpose. In later usage the term also came to denote a plot element introduced early whose significance becomes clear only later (a form of foreshadowing or setup-to-payoff).

Examples:
- James Bond films: gadgets shown early in a mission (for example, a concealed wrist-activated dart gun in Moonraker) typically serve vital roles later; the trope dictates not presenting devices that are not used.
- Arthur Miller, A View from the Bridge: Eddie slices an apple with his pocket-knife in Act I; in Act II, Eddie is stabbed to death with the same knife.

Criticism and counterexamples:
- Ernest Hemingway mocked the principle in his essay "The art of the short story", citing his short story "Fifty Grand" which introduces characters and never mentions them again, arguing for value in inconsequential details while acknowledging readers seek significance.
- Andrea Phillips argued that assigning a single role for every detail makes stories predictable and "colorless".
- Donald Rayfield (1999) noted that Chekhov's own play The Cherry Orchard contains two loaded firearms that are not fired; those unfired rifles relate to the play's theme of lacking or incomplete action.

Related concepts:
- Concision (brevity in writing)
- Foreshadowing (hinting at what is to come)
- MacGuffin (plot motivator important to characters but insignificant in itself)
- Occam's razor (prefer explanations without unnecessary entities)
- Red herring (misleading attention)
- Shaggy dog story (long anecdote that misleads to an anticlimax)
- Deus ex machina (unexpected plot element that resolves a problem)
