## Original Content

Definition: An "unreliable narrator" is "a narrator who cannot be trusted, one whose credibility is compromised."

History / key contribution: Term coined by Wayne C. Booth (1961) in The Rhetoric of Fiction. Booth's position: narrator reliability is judged by whether the narrator's speech conforms to the implied author's norms; Booth wrote that a narrator is "reliable when he speaks for or acts in accordance with the norms of the work."

Classification / types (William Riggan, 1981):
- Picaro: first-person, picaresque antihero whose account contrasts with author/reader perspective. Examples: Apuleius (The Golden Ass); Lazaro (Lazarillo de Tormes); Moll Flanders.
- Clown: fool/jester type using irony, ambiguity, and reversal. Examples: Tristram Shandy; Humbert Humbert (Lolita); Oskar Matzerath (The Tin Drum).
- Madman: narrator untrustworthy due to mental imbalance. Examples: Poprishchin (Diary of a Madman); narrator of Notes from Underground; Poe's first-person narrators.
- Naif: narrator reveals lack of experience or understanding without conscious deception. Examples: Huckleberry Finn; Holden Caulfield (The Catcher in the Rye).

Other theoretical approaches:
- Peter J. Rabinowitz reframed unreliability in terms of different audiences (actual, authorial, narrative, ideal narrative).
- Ansgar Nünning emphasizes reader cognition and definable textual "signals" (intratextual contradictions, extratextual impossibilities, literary competence).
- James Phelan introduced "bonding unreliability" (unreliable narration that creates author-audience bonding).
- Greta Olson proposed treating narrators along spectra of fallibility and untrustworthiness rather than a binary.

Signals of unreliable narration:
- Intratextual: narrator contradicts self, omits facts, lies to characters.
- Extratextual: statements contradict reader's world knowledge or logic.
- Reader competence: genre/stock-character knowledge and literary conventions help detect unreliability.
