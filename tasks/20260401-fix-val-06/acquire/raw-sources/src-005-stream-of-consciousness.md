## Original Content

Definition and technique: Stream of consciousness is a narrative mode that seeks to represent a character's flowing mental life and impressions, usually by presenting interior thought in a form that may be disjointed or irregular in punctuation and structure. It overlaps with interior monologue but can be distinguished: the subject matter is the "stream of consciousness," while the technique presenting it can be an "interior monologue." An interior monologue may present thoughts "directly, without the apparent intervention of a summarizing and selecting narrator," while stream-of-consciousness technique may also mingle impressions and perceptions and sometimes violate grammar or logic.

Origin: The phrase was used earlier (1855) by Alexander Bain in psychological writing, and is commonly credited to William James (1890). James wrote that "consciousness, then, does not appear to itself as chopped up in bits ... it flows." (William James, The Principles of Psychology). The term entered literary usage in April 1918 (May Sinclair, in The Egoist) referring to Dorothy Richardson's Pilgrimage; Richardson herself called the phrase a "lamentably ill-chosen metaphor."

Literary development and practitioners:
- Late-19th-century precursors: Laurence Sterne's Tristram Shandy, John Neal (Seventy-Six), Poe (The Tell-Tale Heart), Tolstoy (moments in War and Peace and Anna Karenina), Ambrose Bierce (An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge), Edouard Dujardin (Les Lauriers sont coupes), Anton Chekhov, Knut Hamsun (Hunger, Mysteries), and Henry James (Portrait of a Lady). Dujardin influenced later writers; Joyce "picked up a copy of Dujardin's novel ... in Paris in 1903."
- The technique was fully developed in modernist fiction in the early 20th century. Dorothy Richardson's Pointed Roofs (1915) is cited as the first complete English stream-of-consciousness novel.
- James Joyce, Marcel Proust, and Virginia Woolf are named as key modernist practitioners. Modernists such as Proust, Joyce, Richardson and Woolf developed the technique "though very differently, simultaneously."
- James Joyce: major employment in A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man, Ulysses (1922), and Finnegans Wake; combines stream of consciousness with authorial description and free indirect style.
- Virginia Woolf: blends omniscient description, free indirect discourse and interior monologue across Mrs Dalloway and To the Lighthouse; Woolf "blurs the distinction between direct and indirect speech."
- Other noted authors: William Faulkner (The Sound and the Fury), Samuel Beckett (Molloy, Malone Dies, The Unnamable), Jean-Paul Sartre (Roads to Freedom trilogy), Malcolm Lowry (Under the Volcano), James Kelman, Salman Rushdie, Sylvia Plath (The Bell Jar), Irvine Welsh (Trainspotting), Dave Eggers, Roberto Bolano.

Examples and style notes: Joyce passage as example of interior monologue; "what kind of flowers are those" (from Molly Bloom passage). T. S. Eliot's "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock" (1915) noted as using interior-monologue techniques.
