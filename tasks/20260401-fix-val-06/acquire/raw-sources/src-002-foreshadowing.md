## Original Content

"Foreshadowing is a narrative device in which suggestions or warnings about events to come are dropped or planted."

Purpose: Writers use foreshadowing to build anticipation, create suspense, make unlikely events feel credible, and prepare readers for future plot developments or revelations.

Implementation: It can appear in dialogue, plot events, settings, chapter or work titles, and musical leitmotifs in staged/filmed works. It frequently occurs early in a story to shape or subvert audience expectations.

Effect on pacing: Authors may delay plot progression or include cliffhangers to heighten the impression that something significant will happen.

Examples:
- In the Book of Genesis, Joseph dreams of his brothers' grain bundles bowing to his own. Later on, when Joseph becomes vizier of Egypt, his brothers bow to him as hinted by the dream.

Methods / related narrative devices:
- Flashback: A flashback is the interruption of a sequential narrative plot to present important events that have happened in the past. The article notes flashbacks are used to present past events, traits, or themes relevant to the current narrative.
- Sideshadowing: introduced via Gary Morson's criticism; described as including scenes that ultimately have no plot relevance to increase realism and invite interpretation.
- Relationship/contrast with similar devices:
  - Red herring — a hint meant to mislead; foreshadowing hints toward an outcome within the narrative.
  - Flashforward — moves the narrative forward in time; foreshadowing hints without necessarily showing the future scene.
  - Chekhov's gun — a discipline of economy in storytelling that expects introduced elements to later matter.
  - Sideshadowing — intentionally includes inconsequential scenes to mimic real life and complicate interpretation.
