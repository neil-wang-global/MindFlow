## Topics Acquired

### 1. Foreshadowing and Chekhov's Gun (ACQ-001)
- Foreshadowing: narrative device planting hints about future events to build anticipation and suspense
- Chekhov's Gun: dramatic principle (Anton Chekhov, 1889 letter) requiring every narrative element to be necessary — "If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired"
- Related concepts: red herring (misleading foreshadowing), sideshadowing (inconsequential details for realism), MacGuffin
- Sources: Wikipedia articles on Chekhov's gun and Foreshadowing, citing Chekhov's letters, Ginsburg (1997), Morson (1998)

### 2. Unreliable Narrator (ACQ-002)
- Definition: narrator whose credibility is compromised (Wayne C. Booth, The Rhetoric of Fiction, 1961)
- Taxonomy (William Riggan, 1981): Picaro, Clown, Madman, Naif
- Later frameworks: Phelan's bonding unreliability, Nünning's cognitive approach, Olson's fallibility/untrustworthiness spectrum
- Signals: intratextual contradictions, extratextual impossibilities, reader literary competence
- Sources: Wikipedia article on Unreliable narrator, citing Booth (1961), Riggan (1981), Phelan (2007), Nünning (1998), Olson (2003)

### 3. Stream of Consciousness (ACQ-003)
- Definition: narrative mode representing a character's flowing mental life and impressions
- Origin: term coined by William James (The Principles of Psychology, 1890) — "consciousness does not appear to itself as chopped up in bits ... it flows"
- Literary entry: May Sinclair (1918) applied term to Dorothy Richardson's Pilgrimage
- Key practitioners: James Joyce (Ulysses, 1922), Virginia Woolf (Mrs Dalloway, To the Lighthouse), William Faulkner (The Sound and the Fury)
- Distinction: subject matter = stream of consciousness; technique = interior monologue
- Sources: Wikipedia article on Stream of consciousness (narrative mode), citing James (1890), Humphrey (1954), Cohn (1978), Friedman (1955)

### 4. Frame Narrative (ACQ-004)
- Definition: literary technique where an outer narrative establishes context for embedded inner tales
- Ancient origins: Egyptian literature (Papyrus Westcar), Indian literature (Panchatantra, Mahabharata)
- Classic examples: One Thousand and One Nights (Scheherazade), Canterbury Tales (Chaucer), Decameron (Boccaccio), Frankenstein (Shelley — triple nesting)
- Structural mechanics: organizing device, positioning reader attitude, false documents, procatalepsis, mise en abyme
- Sources: Wikipedia article on Frame story, citing Ginsburg (1997), Witzel (1987), Irwin (1998)
