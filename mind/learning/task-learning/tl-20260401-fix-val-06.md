### [KDC-001] Chekhov's Gun Principle

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-001
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-fix-val-06/acquire/raw-sources/src-001-chekhovs-gun.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Chekhov's gun is a narrative principle that \"every element in a story [be] necessary, while irrelevant elements should be removed.\" It is recorded in several letters by Anton Chekhov as advice to young playwrights (Chekhov letters cited, e.g. 1 November 1889)."
- **Derived Conclusion**: Chekhov's gun is a dramatic principle of narrative economy attributed to Anton Chekhov (formalized in his 1889 letter to Lazarev), requiring that every element introduced in a story serve a necessary purpose — if a gun is shown on the wall in Act 1, it must be fired by Act 3.

### [KDC-002] Foreshadowing as Narrative Device

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-001
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-fix-val-06/acquire/raw-sources/src-002-foreshadowing.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Foreshadowing is a narrative device in which suggestions or warnings about events to come are dropped or planted."
- **Derived Conclusion**: Foreshadowing is a narrative device that plants hints about future events to build anticipation, create suspense, and make unlikely events feel credible. It operates through dialogue, plot events, settings, and titles. Related but distinct from Chekhov's gun (which demands necessity) and red herring (which deliberately misleads).

### [KDC-003] Unreliable Narrator Concept and Taxonomy

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-002
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-fix-val-06/acquire/raw-sources/src-003-unreliable-narrator.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Definition: An \"unreliable narrator\" is \"a narrator who cannot be trusted, one whose credibility is compromised.\" History / key contribution: Term coined by Wayne C. Booth (1961) in The Rhetoric of Fiction. Booth's position: narrator reliability is judged by whether the narrator's speech conforms to the implied author's norms; Booth wrote that a narrator is \"reliable when he speaks for or acts in accordance with the norms of the work.\""
- **Derived Conclusion**: The unreliable narrator is a literary concept coined by Wayne C. Booth (1961) in which a narrator's credibility is compromised. Riggan (1981) classified unreliable narrators into four types: Picaro, Clown, Madman, and Naif. Later scholars expanded the framework: Nünning emphasized reader cognition, Phelan introduced bonding unreliability, and Olson proposed a fallibility/untrustworthiness spectrum.

### [KDC-004] Stream of Consciousness Literary Origins

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-003
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-fix-val-06/acquire/raw-sources/src-005-stream-of-consciousness.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Origin: The phrase was used earlier (1855) by Alexander Bain in psychological writing, and is commonly credited to William James (1890). James wrote that \"consciousness, then, does not appear to itself as chopped up in bits ... it flows.\" (William James, The Principles of Psychology). The term entered literary usage in April 1918 (May Sinclair, in The Egoist) referring to Dorothy Richardson's Pilgrimage; Richardson herself called the phrase a \"lamentably ill-chosen metaphor.\""
- **Derived Conclusion**: Stream of consciousness originated as a psychological concept (William James, 1890) describing the continuous flow of thought, then entered literary usage via May Sinclair (1918) applied to Dorothy Richardson's work. The distinction is important: stream of consciousness is the subject matter (the flow of thought), while interior monologue is the technique for presenting it. Key practitioners include Joyce (Ulysses), Woolf (Mrs Dalloway), and Faulkner (The Sound and the Fury).

### [KDC-005] Frame Narrative Structure and Mechanics

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-004
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-fix-val-06/acquire/raw-sources/src-006-frame-story.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Definition: A frame story (also called frame tale, framing device, frame narrative, sandwich narrative, or intercalation) is a literary technique in which an introductory or main narrative establishes the setting and context for one or more embedded tales. The outer narrative leads readers into the inner narratives and can supply information that clarifies them."
- **Derived Conclusion**: Frame narrative is a literary structure where an outer story provides context for embedded inner tales. Originating in ancient Egyptian and Indian literature, it reached its classic form in One Thousand and One Nights (Scheherazade), Canterbury Tales (Chaucer), and Decameron (Boccaccio). Structural mechanics include: organizing collections, positioning reader attitude, casting doubt on narrators, false documents, and mise en abyme (recursive self-embedding).
