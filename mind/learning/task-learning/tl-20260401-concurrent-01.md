### [KDC-002] In Medias Res Technique

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-001
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-concurrent-01/acquire/raw-sources/src-001-narrative-structure.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "In medias res (Latin: \"into the middle of things\") is a narrative technique in which the story begins at a midpoint or climactic moment, rather than at the chronological beginning."
- **Derived Conclusion**: In medias res is a narrative technique opening at a midpoint or climactic moment rather than the chronological beginning, first described by Horace (c. 19 BCE), creating immediate engagement through action, deferred exposition, flashbacks, and urgency.

### [KDC-003] Rhetorical Device Taxonomy: Metaphor, Anaphora, Parallelism, Antithesis

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-002
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-concurrent-01/acquire/raw-sources/src-003-rhetorical-devices.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "Anaphora is the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses, sentences, or lines. It creates rhythm and emphasis, building rhetorical momentum."
- **Derived Conclusion**: Rhetorical devices are classified into figures of comparison (metaphor, simile, metonymy), figures of repetition (anaphora, epistrophe), and figures of balance (parallelism, antithesis, chiasmus). Anaphora creates rhetorical momentum through beginning-of-clause repetition; parallelism creates balance through grammatical symmetry; antithesis creates emphasis through juxtaposition of opposites in parallel structure.

### [KDC-004] Aristotelian Appeals: Ethos, Pathos, Logos

**Source Type**: grounded-external

- **Acquisition Event**: ACQ-003
- **Source Anchor**: `tasks/20260401-concurrent-01/acquire/raw-sources/src-005-persuasion-purdue.md`
- **Verification Status**: passed
- **Original Excerpt**: > "The three primary modes of persuasion — ethos, pathos, and logos — were first articulated by Aristotle in his *Rhetoric* and remain the foundational framework for analyzing and producing persuasive writing."
- **Derived Conclusion**: Aristotle's three modes of persuasion form the foundational framework for persuasive writing: ethos (credibility, established through expertise and fairness), pathos (emotional appeal through vivid language and narrative), and logos (logical appeal through deductive/inductive reasoning and evidence). The enthymeme is "the body of persuasion" — a rhetorical syllogism engaging the audience by requiring them to supply the missing premise.
