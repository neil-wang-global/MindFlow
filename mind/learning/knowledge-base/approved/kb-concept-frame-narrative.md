## Summary
- Frame narrative is a literary structure where an outer story provides context for embedded inner tales. Originating in ancient Egyptian and Indian literature, it reached its classic form in One Thousand and One Nights (Scheherazade), Canterbury Tales (Chaucer), and Decameron (Boccaccio). Structural mechanics include: organizing collections, positioning reader attitude, casting doubt on narrators, false documents, and mise en abyme (recursive self-embedding).

## Key Evidence
> "Definition: A frame story (also called frame tale, framing device, frame narrative, sandwich narrative, or intercalation) is a literary technique in which an introductory or main narrative establishes the setting and context for one or more embedded tales. The outer narrative leads readers into the inner narratives and can supply information that clarifies them."

## Applicability
- Applies to: literary analysis, narrative structure design, story collection organization, meta-fiction techniques, nested narrative analysis
- Does not apply to: simple flashback sequences (which are temporal shifts, not frame structures); should not be confused with point-of-view shifts within a single continuous narrative

## Notes
- Mise en abyme (story-within-a-story-within-a-story) is a special recursive case of frame narrative, seen in works like Hamlet's play-within-a-play
