## Summary
- Stream of consciousness originated as a psychological concept (William James, 1890) describing the continuous flow of thought, then entered literary usage via May Sinclair (1918) applied to Dorothy Richardson's work. The distinction is important: stream of consciousness is the subject matter (the flow of thought), while interior monologue is the technique for presenting it. Key practitioners include Joyce (Ulysses), Woolf (Mrs Dalloway), and Faulkner (The Sound and the Fury).

## Key Evidence
> "Origin: The phrase was used earlier (1855) by Alexander Bain in psychological writing, and is commonly credited to William James (1890). James wrote that \"consciousness, then, does not appear to itself as chopped up in bits ... it flows.\" (William James, The Principles of Psychology). The term entered literary usage in April 1918 (May Sinclair, in The Egoist) referring to Dorothy Richardson's Pilgrimage; Richardson herself called the phrase a \"lamentably ill-chosen metaphor.\""

## Applicability
- Applies to: literary analysis of modernist fiction, narrative technique classification, writing style analysis, comparative literature (Joyce/Woolf/Faulkner traditions)
- Does not apply to: informal uses of "stream of consciousness" to describe any unstructured writing; should not be equated with interior monologue (which is the technique, not the subject matter)

## Notes
- The term/concept distinction (stream of consciousness as subject matter vs. interior monologue as technique) is frequently conflated in casual literary discussion
