## Original Excerpt
> "Definition: An \"unreliable narrator\" is \"a narrator who cannot be trusted, one whose credibility is compromised.\" History / key contribution: Term coined by Wayne C. Booth (1961) in The Rhetoric of Fiction. Booth's position: narrator reliability is judged by whether the narrator's speech conforms to the implied author's norms; Booth wrote that a narrator is \"reliable when he speaks for or acts in accordance with the norms of the work.\""

## Candidate Conclusion
- The unreliable narrator is a literary concept coined by Wayne C. Booth (1961) in which a narrator's credibility is compromised. Riggan (1981) classified unreliable narrators into four types: Picaro, Clown, Madman, and Naif. Later scholars expanded the framework: Nünning emphasized reader cognition, Phelan introduced bonding unreliability, and Olson proposed a fallibility/untrustworthiness spectrum.
